OVERVIEW: CARDIOVASCULAR COMPLAINT CLUSTERS
-------------------------------------------
1) Chest pain / chest discomfort
2) Shortness of breath (dyspnea), orthopnea, PND
3) Palpitations (fast / irregular / pounding / skipping)
4) Syncope / near-syncope (passing out / almost passing out)
5) Leg swelling (edema)
6) Exertional leg pain (claudication)
7) Atypical anginal equivalents (jaw pain, epigastric pain, “indigestion”, isolated dyspnea)
8) Known heart failure / “fluid overload” check-ins
9) “High blood pressure” visits (hypertensive symptoms screen)
10) “Fast heart rate” / tachycardia noted on device
11) “Skipped beats” / extra beats felt by patient

NOTE:
- These questions + answers are to guide HealthYoda’s structured intake.
- HealthYoda MUST NOT diagnose or suggest treatment.
- Use these to normalize and structure what patients say.

SECTION 1 — UNIVERSAL CARDIO HPI STRUCTURE (WITH ANSWER EXAMPLES)
================================================================

ONSET — “When did this start? Did it begin suddenly or gradually?”
- “It started suddenly about 20 minutes ago.”
- “I woke up with this feeling this morning.”
- “It’s been gradually getting worse over the past 3 weeks.”
- “It’s been on and off for months.”
- “It started in the middle of my workout.”

DURATION — “How long does each episode last?”
- “Only a few seconds at a time.”
- “Each episode lasts about 10 minutes.”
- “It’s constant all day but has ups and downs.”
- “It comes and goes randomly through the day.”

SEVERITY — “On a scale from 0 to 10, how bad is it at its worst?”
- “Around a 2, mild.”
- “About a 6 out of 10.”
- “When it’s bad, it’s 9 out of 10.”
- “Constant 4, but sometimes shoots up to 8.”

TRIGGERS / CONTEXT — “What seems to bring it on or make it worse?”
- “Walking or climbing stairs makes it worse.”
- “It gets worse when I’m stressed or anxious.”
- “It seems to happen after big meals.”
- “It’s worse when I lie flat.”
- “Happens when I bend over.”
- “Sometimes seems random.”

RELIEVING FACTORS — “What makes it better?”
- “Resting for a few minutes helps.”
- “Sitting up helps.”
- “Leaning forward makes it a bit better.”
- “Antacids help slightly.”
- “Nothing seems to help.”

ASSOCIATED SYMPTOMS — “When this happens, do any of these occur?”
- “I get sweaty.”
- “I feel nauseated.”
- “I feel lightheaded, like I might pass out.”
- “My left arm gets numb or heavy.”
- “I feel short of breath.”
- “My heart feels like it’s racing or pounding.”

RISK FACTORS (simple yes/no screening)
- “I have high blood pressure.”
- “I have diabetes.”
- “My cholesterol is high.”
- “I smoke / I used to smoke.”
- “I had a heart attack before.”
- “My father had a heart attack in his 40s.”
- “I don’t have any known medical problems.”

SECTION 2 — CHEST PAIN / CHEST DISCOMFORT
===============================================================

Q1. “When did this chest discomfort or pain start?”
- “About 20 minutes ago while I was walking.”
- “Yesterday evening.”
- “A few weeks ago, off and on.”
- “It woke me up from sleep last night.”

Q2. “Where exactly do you feel it? Can you point to the main spot?”
- “Right in the middle of my chest.”
- “A bit to the left side.”
- “Under my breastbone.”
- “In the upper part of my stomach.”

Q3. “Does the discomfort move anywhere, like into your arm, jaw, neck, back, or shoulder?”
- “Yes, it goes down my left arm.”
- “It radiates into my jaw and neck.”
- “It shoots through to my back between my shoulder blades.”
- “No, it stays in one place.”

Q4. “How would you describe the discomfort: pressure, tightness, squeezing, sharp, stabbing, burning, or something else?”
- “Feels like a heavy pressure.”
- “Like someone is squeezing my chest.”
- “Sharp stabbing pain when I take a deep breath.”
- “Burning feeling after I eat.”
- “Feels like a tight band around my chest.”

Q5. “On a scale from 0 to 10, how bad is the pain at its worst?”
- “Right now it’s about a 3.”
- “At its worst it was a 7.”
- “It shot up to a 9 earlier.”
- “Constant 4, but spikes higher when I move.”

Q6. “Does it get worse when you walk, climb stairs, or exert yourself?”
- “Yes, walking even a short distance makes it worse.”
- “Climbing stairs definitely triggers it.”
- “No, it happens even when I’m resting.”
- “Only happens when I’m exerting myself hard.”

Q7. “Does it get better when you sit or rest?”
- “Yes, it usually eases up after a few minutes of rest.”
- “Sometimes it gets a bit better but doesn’t go away.”
- “No, resting doesn’t change it.”

Q8. “Does taking a deep breath make it worse?”
- “Yes, the pain is sharper when I breathe in deeply.”
- “It hurts when I cough or sneeze.”
- “No, deep breaths don’t change it.”

Q9. “Does changing your position (like lying down, rolling over, or leaning forward) make it better or worse?”
- “Lying flat makes it worse.”
- “Leaning forward makes it a little better.”
- “Changing position doesn’t make a difference.”

Q10. “When this pain happens, do you get sweaty, nauseated, or feel like you might pass out?”
- “Yes, I was drenched in sweat when it started.”
- “I felt very nauseated and thought I might vomit.”
- “I got really lightheaded, like I was going to pass out.”
- “No, I don’t feel any of that.”

Q11. “Do you feel short of breath when the pain comes?”
- “Yes, I can’t catch my breath.”
- “A little bit, but not too bad.”
- “No, breathing is normal.”

Q12. “Does it seem related to food, like after big meals or certain foods?”
- “Yes, happens mainly after heavy or spicy meals.”
- “Worse when I lie down after eating.”
- “No, I haven’t noticed any connection to food.”

Q13. “Have you ever had a similar pain before?”
- “Yes, I had something similar last year.”
- “I get this pain once or twice a week.”
- “No, this is the first time I’ve ever felt this.”

Q14. “Do you have high blood pressure, diabetes, high cholesterol, or any history of heart disease or heart procedures?”
- “I have high blood pressure and take medication.”
- “I’m diabetic.”
- “My cholesterol is high.”
- “I had a stent put in two years ago.”
- “No, I’ve never had any heart problems diagnosed.”


RED-FLAG PATTERNS FOR CHEST PAIN (logic for the system, not spoken):
- New chest pain that is:
  - Pressure or squeezing
  - Worse with exertion
  - Better with rest
  - With radiation to arm/jaw/back
  - With sweating, nausea, or sense of impending doom
- Sudden, very severe “ripping/tearing” pain radiating to the back
- Chest pain with shortness of breath at rest, or with syncope

SECTION 3 — SHORTNESS OF BREATH / ORTHOPNEA / PND
===============================================================

Q1. “When did the shortness of breath start?”
- “It started suddenly about an hour ago.”
- “It’s been slowly getting worse over the last week.”
- “I’ve noticed it for months but it’s worse now.”

Q2. “Do you feel short of breath mainly when you’re at rest, when you’re active, or both?”
- “Only when I walk or go up stairs.”
- “I feel short of breath even when I’m just sitting.”
- “I only notice it when I lie down.”

Q3. “How far can you walk before you have to stop because of shortness of breath?”
- “Just to the bathroom before I need to rest.”
- “Half a flight of stairs.”
- “I can walk around the house but not outdoors.”
- “I can walk several blocks without a problem.”

Q4. “Do you feel short of breath when you lie flat?”
- “Yes, I can’t lie flat at all.”
- “I need two or three pillows to sleep.”
- “No, lying flat doesn’t bother me.”

Q5. “Do you ever wake up suddenly at night feeling short of breath or gasping for air?”
- “Yes, it happens a couple of times a week.”
- “It happened for the first time this week.”
- “No, I sleep fine.”

Q6. “Do you have any cough? If so, what does it look like?”
- “Yes, I cough up clear mucus.”
- “Yes, I coughed up pink, frothy mucus once.”
- “Just a dry cough.”
- “No cough at all.”

Q7. “Any swelling in your legs, ankles, or feet?”
- “Yes, both ankles are swollen by the end of the day.”
- “My right leg is more swollen than the left.”
- “No swelling that I’ve noticed.”

Q8. “Any chest pain or tightness with the shortness of breath?”
- “Yes, I get tightness in my chest when walking.”
- “Sometimes a mild pressure when I’m really short of breath.”
- “No chest pain, just difficulty breathing.”

Q9. “Any fever, chills, or recent illness?”
- “I had a fever and cough last week.”
- “No fever or chills, just breathing problems.”


RED-FLAG PATTERNS FOR DYSPNEA:
- Sudden severe shortness of breath at rest
- Shortness of breath with chest pain, especially at rest
- Shortness of breath where the patient can’t speak full sentences
- Pink frothy sputum
- Blue lips or fingertips (if described)

SECTION 4 — PALPITATIONS (FAST / IRREGULAR HEARTBEATS)
===============================================================

Q1. “What does your heartbeat feel like when this happens?”
- “It feels like my heart is racing really fast.”
- “It’s pounding out of my chest.”
- “It flutters or skips beats.”
- “It feels like fish flopping in my chest.”

Q2. “Do these episodes start suddenly, like a light switch, or do they build up slowly?”
- “It starts very suddenly, all at once.”
- “It gradually speeds up.”
- “Sometimes sudden, sometimes gradual.”

Q3. “How long do the episodes usually last?”
- “Just a few seconds.”
- “Usually a few minutes.”
- “Last time it went on for an hour.”

Q4. “How often do you notice these episodes?”
- “Once or twice a day.”
- “A few times a week.”
- “Maybe once a month.”

Q5. “What seems to bring them on?”
- “Caffeine or energy drinks.”
- “Stress or anxiety.”
- “Exercise.”
- “They seem to come out of nowhere.”

Q6. “When this happens, do you feel dizzy, lightheaded, or like you might pass out?”
- “Yes, I almost blacked out once.”
- “I feel lightheaded but don’t pass out.”
- “No dizziness.”

Q7. “Any chest discomfort or shortness of breath with the palpitations?”
- “Yes, chest pressure when my heart races.”
- “I feel short of breath when it’s very fast.”
- “No chest pain and no SOB.”


RED-FLAG PATTERNS FOR PALPITATIONS:
- Palpitations with syncope or near syncope
- Palpitations with chest pain
- Very fast heart rate at rest with dizziness or confusion


===============================================================
SECTION 5 — SYNCOPE / NEAR-SYNCOPE
===============================================================

Q1. “Did you fully pass out (lose consciousness), or did you just feel like you were about to faint?”
- “I completely blacked out.”
- “I didn’t actually faint but felt like I would.”
- “My vision went dark but I managed to sit down.”

Q2. “What were you doing right before this happened?”
- “Standing up quickly from bed.”
- “Walking to the bathroom.”
- “Climbing the stairs.”
- “Just sitting watching TV.”

Q3. “Did you notice any warning signs before you passed out or felt like you would?”
- “Yes, I felt sweaty and lightheaded.”
- “My vision started to tunnel.”
- “My heart was racing first.”
- “No, it happened without warning.”

Q4. “Do you remember the event, or is there a gap in your memory?”
- “There’s a blank for a few seconds.”
- “I remember starting to fall.”
- “I don’t remember anything until I woke up.”

Q5. “How quickly did you recover once you came around?”
- “Almost immediately, I felt okay.”
- “I was confused for a minute or two.”
- “I still feel weak and lightheaded.”

Q6. “Did you injure yourself when you fell?”
- “Yes, I hit my head.”
- “I landed on my side but nothing serious.”
- “No injury, I caught myself.”

RED-FLAG PATTERNS FOR SYNCOPE:
- Syncope during exertion
- Syncope preceded by chest pain or palpitations
- Syncope with no warning symptoms
- Syncope with family history of sudden cardiac death

SECTION 6 — LEG SWELLING (EDEMA)
===============================================================

Q1. “Is one leg swollen or both?”
- “Both ankles are swollen.”
- “Only my right leg is swollen.”
- “My left calf is bigger and feels tight.”

Q2. “When did you first notice the swelling?”
- “Gradually over the past few weeks.”
- “Suddenly this morning.”
- “I’m not sure, but my shoes feel tighter recently.”

Q3. “Is the swollen area painful, red, or warm to touch?”
- “Yes, it’s painful and warm.”
- “Feels tight but not very painful.”
- “No pain, just puffy.”

Q4. “Does the swelling get worse as the day goes on?”
- “Yes, it’s much worse by evening.”
- “Stays about the same.”
- “Hard to tell.”

Q5. “Does elevating your legs help reduce the swelling?”
- “Yes, when I put my feet up it improves.”
- “Only a little better.”
- “No real change.”

Q6. “Any shortness of breath or chest discomfort along with the swelling?”
- “Yes, I get short of breath, especially lying down.”
- “I feel out of breath when walking.”
- “No, breathing is fine.”


RED-FLAG PATTERNS FOR LEG SWELLING:
- Sudden one-sided swelling with pain/redness
- Leg swelling with new or worsening shortness of breath or chest pain
- Rapid generalized swelling with breathing difficulty

SECTION 7 — EXERTIONAL LEG PAIN (CLAUDICATION)
===============================================================

Q1. “Do you get pain in your legs when you walk?”
- “Yes, my calves hurt when I walk a block.”
- “My thighs cramp when I go uphill.”
- “No leg pain with walking.”

Q2. “Where do you feel the pain: calves, thighs, buttocks?”
- “Mostly in my calves.”
- “In my thighs.”
- “In my buttocks when I walk far.”

Q3. “Does the pain go away when you stop and rest?”
- “Yes, it goes away after a few minutes of rest.”
- “It eases but doesn’t fully go away.”
- “No, the pain persists even at rest.”

Q4. “Has this been getting worse over time?”
- “Yes, I used to walk farther before it started.”
- “About the same for months.”
- “Just recently appeared.”


RED-FLAG PATTERNS:
- Leg pain at rest with cold/pale foot
- Sudden onset severe leg pain with color change

SECTION 8 — ATYPICAL ANGINAL EQUIVALENTS
===============================================================

Examples of Anginal-Equivalent Presentations:
- Epigastric “indigestion” type discomfort with exertion
- Isolated jaw or neck pain with exertion
- Isolated shortness of breath with exertion (especially in diabetics and older adults)

Q1. “Does your ‘indigestion’ or stomach discomfort come on with activity and improve with rest?”
- “Yes, it happens when I walk and settles down when I stop.”
- “Only after I eat, not with walking.”
- “Happens both with walking and after meals.”

Q2. “Does your jaw or neck pain appear when you exert yourself and go away when you rest?”
- “Yes, my jaw aches when I climb stairs.”
- “Neck pain only after exercise.”
- “No relation to exercise.”

Q3. “Do you ever get short of breath with activity but no chest pain?”
- “Yes, I only feel winded but no chest pain.”
- “I get tired but not exactly short of breath.”
- “No, I don’t get breathless with activity.”

SECTION 9 — KNOWN HEART FAILURE / FLUID OVERLOAD CHECK-IN
===============================================================

For patients who say “I have heart failure” or “my heart is weak”:

Q1. “Have you noticed your weight increasing quickly over the last few days?”
- “Yes, I gained 3–4 kilos in a week.”
- “My clothes feel tighter.”
- “No weight gain that I know of.”

Q2. “Are your shoes or rings fitting tighter than usual?”
- “Yes, my shoes are tighter at the end of the day.”
- “My rings are hard to remove now.”
- “No noticeable change.”

Q3. “Any change in how many pillows you need to sleep comfortably?”
- “I used to sleep flat, now I need 3 pillows.”
- “I now have to sleep in a chair.”
- “No change in pillows.”

Q4. “Any new or worsening swelling in your legs or belly?”
- “My legs are much more swollen.”
- “My belly feels more bloated.”
- “No new swelling.”


RED-FLAG PATTERNS:
- Rapid weight gain + marked edema + severe orthopnea/PND
- Worsening dyspnea at rest


===============================================================
SECTION 10 — ‘HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE’ VISITS (SYMPTOM SCREEN)
===============================================================

If patient says: “My blood pressure is high” or “device showed high BP”:

Q1. “Are you having any symptoms right now such as headache, vision changes, chest pain, or shortness of breath?”
- “Yes, I have a severe headache.”
- “My vision is blurry.”
- “I have chest pressure.”
- “I feel very short of breath.”
- “No, I feel okay, just saw a high reading.”

Q2. “Any weakness, numbness, trouble speaking, or trouble understanding others?”
- “My right arm feels weak.”
- “Words are hard to get out.”
- “No, my movement and speech are normal.”

Q3. “Did you measure your blood pressure more than once?”
- “Yes, it was high three times.”
- “Only once.”
- “At the pharmacy it was high.”


RED-FLAG PATTERNS:
- High BP + chest pain
- High BP + neurologic symptoms (weakness, speech difficulty, vision loss)
- High BP + acute shortness of breath


===============================================================
SECTION 11 — ‘FAST HEART RATE’ / TACHYCARDIA ON DEVICE
===============================================================

If patient says: “My watch says my heart rate is very high.”

Q1. “What heart rate number did you see?”
- “It went up to 160.”
- “Frequently over 120 while resting.”
- “Only high with exercise.”

Q2. “Were you resting, walking, or exercising when it was that high?”
- “I was just sitting.”
- “I had just walked up stairs.”
- “I was doing a workout.”

Q3. “Any chest discomfort, shortness of breath, dizziness, or near-fainting when it was fast?”
- “Yes, I felt lightheaded.”
- “Chest tightness and I had to sit down.”
- “No symptoms, I just saw the number.”


RED-FLAG PATTERNS:
- Resting tachycardia with symptoms
- Very high rate with chest pain, SOB, or syncope


===============================================================
SECTION 12 — ‘SKIPPED BEATS’ / EXTRA BEATS
===============================================================

Q1. “What does it feel like when your heart skips a beat?”
- “Feels like a pause and then a stronger beat.”
- “Like my heart drops for a second then thumps.”
- “Little flutters every now and then.”

Q2. “How often do you feel this?”
- “A few times a day.”
- “Once in a while, maybe weekly.”
- “Many times every hour.”

Q3. “Any chest pain, shortness of breath, or dizziness with these skipped beats?”
- “No, just the sensation.”
- “I occasionally feel slightly lightheaded.”
- “Yes, I almost faint with them.”

RED-FLAG PATTERNS:
- Frequent skipped beats with syncope or near syncope
- Extra beats with chest pain or significant dyspnea
