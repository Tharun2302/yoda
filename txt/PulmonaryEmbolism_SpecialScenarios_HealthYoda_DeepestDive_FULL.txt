SECTION 1 — CLASSIC PE PRESENTATION
===================================================================

CORE TRIAD (NOT ALWAYS ALL PRESENT):
- Sudden onset shortness of breath
- Pleuritic chest pain (sharp, worse on deep breath)
- Tachycardia (+/- cough, hemoptysis)

Q1. When did your symptoms start?
A:
- “Very suddenly a few hours ago.”
- “Came on quickly this morning.”

Q2. Describe your breathing.
A:
- “Feels like I can’t get enough air.”
- “Short of breath, even at rest.”

Q3. Chest pain?
A:
- “Sharp pain when I take a deep breath.”
- “Pain on the side of my chest.”

Q4. Cough or blood?
A:
- “Dry cough.”
- “Saw streaks of blood in my sputum.”

CLASSIC PE RED FLAGS:
- Sudden SOB of unclear cause
- Pleuritic chest pain + risk factors
- Tachycardia + hypoxia
- Hemoptysis with SOB

SECTION 2 — MASSIVE VS SUBMASSIVE VS LOW-RISK PE (SYMPTOM-LEVEL)
===================================================================

MASSIVE PE (hemodynamic instability):
- Hypotension
- Syncope
- Shock
- Confusion
- Cold, clammy skin
- Severe SOB

Q1. Any fainting or near-fainting?
A:
- “Passed out briefly.”
- “Almost blacked out.”

SUBMASSIVE PE (RV strain, but normal BP):
- SOB at rest
- Chest pain
- Tachycardia
- Anxiety, sense of doom

LOW-RISK PE:
- Stable vitals
- Pleuritic pain + SOB on exertion
- Mild or no hypoxia

RED FLAGS FOR MASSIVE PE:
- Low blood pressure
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Chest pain + severe SOB
- Cold, sweaty, grayish appearance

===================================================================
SECTION 3 — ATYPICAL PE PRESENTATIONS
===================================================================

ATYPICAL SYMPTOMS:
- Isolated mild dyspnea
- Unexplained tachycardia
- Isolated syncope without chest pain
- Epigastric or back pain instead of chest pain
- Anxiety/panic-like sensation

Q1. Any history of recent travel, surgery, or immobilization?
A:
- “Long flight.”
- “Recent surgery.”

Q2. Any leg swelling or pain?
A:
- “One calf hurts and is bigger.”

RED FLAGS:
- “Panic attack” with risk factors and unexplained tachycardia
- Syncope in presence of DVT risk
- Unexplained worsening SOB in cancer/pregnancy/post-op patients

===================================================================
SECTION 4 — PE WITH MINIMAL OR NO CHEST PAIN
===================================================================

CLUES:
- Progressive breathlessness
- Unexplained tachycardia
- Lightheadedness
- Anxiety or “air hunger”
- Sometimes only fatigue and decreased exercise tolerance

Q1. Do you have chest pain?
A:
- “No, just very short of breath.”

Q2. Can you climb usual stairs?
A:
- “No, I have to stop halfway now.”

RED FLAGS:
- Rapid drop in exercise tolerance
- Resting SOB without known lung/heart disease
- Tachycardia at rest with SOB

===================================================================
SECTION 5 — PE IN PREGNANCY & POSTPARTUM
===================================================================

CLUES:
- Pregnancy or within 6 weeks postpartum
- SOB beyond expected for pregnancy
- Pleuritic chest pain
- Tachycardia not explained by pregnancy alone
- Leg swelling or pain

Q1. Stage of pregnancy / postpartum?
A:
- “Third trimester.”
- “3 weeks after delivery.”

Q2. Symptoms?
A:
- “Suddenly out of breath just walking across room.”
- “Sharp chest pain with deep breath.”

Q3. Leg symptoms?
A:
- “One calf is painful and swollen.”

RED FLAGS:
- Sudden SOB + pleuritic pain in pregnancy/postpartum
- Syncope in pregnancy
- Severe tachycardia + hypoxia

===================================================================
SECTION 6 — PE IN CANCER PATIENTS
===================================================================

CLUES:
- Known malignancy
- Recent chemo or hormonal therapy
- Central venous catheters
- Immobility
- New or worsening SOB/chest pain

Q1. Current cancer or treatment?
A:
- “On chemo.”
- “Recent surgery for tumor.”

Q2. Symptoms?
A:
- “New shortness of breath.”
- “Chest discomfort on breathing.”

RED FLAGS:
- Acute SOB in known cancer patient
- Chest pain + hemoptysis in cancer
- New tachycardia

===================================================================
SECTION 7 — POST-OPERATIVE & IMMOBILIZATION-RELATED PE
===================================================================

RISK CONTEXTS:
- Surgery (especially orthopedic, abdominal, pelvic)
- Long flights/car rides
- Bed rest/hospitalization
- Casting of limbs

Q1. Recent surgery or prolonged travel?
A:
- “Had knee replacement 1 week ago.”
- “Returned from 10-hour flight yesterday.”

Q2. Symptoms onset?
A:
- “Shortly after I got home.”
- “Day after the long trip.”

RED FLAGS:
- SOB + chest pain within days of surgery/travel
- Syncope post-op
- New unilateral leg swelling + SOB

===================================================================
SECTION 8 — PE ASSOCIATED WITH DVT SYMPTOMS
===================================================================

DVT CLUES:
- Unilateral leg swelling
- Calf or thigh pain
- Leg warmth or discoloration
- Tightness feeling

Q1. Any leg swelling or pain?
A:
- “Right leg more swollen and sore.”

Q2. When did leg symptoms start?
A:
- “A few days before breathing got worse.”

RED FLAGS:
- New SOB in patient with known DVT
- DVT symptoms + chest pain/SOB

===================================================================
SECTION 9 — CHRONIC THROMBOEMBOLIC PULMONARY HYPERTENSION (CTEPH)
===================================================================

CLUES:
- History of prior PE
- Persistent or progressive exertional dyspnea months after PE
- Fatigue
- Leg swelling
- Syncope or presyncope with exertion

Q1. Prior PE history?
A:
- “Had a clot in lung last year.”

Q2. Current symptoms?
A:
- “Still short of breath walking up stairs.”
- “Get lightheaded with exertion.”

RED FLAGS:
- Exertional syncope
- Worsening exercise tolerance after prior PE
- Signs of right heart strain (edema, abdominal distension)

===================================================================
SECTION 10 — RECURRENT PE / HYPERCOAGULABLE STATES
===================================================================

RISK FACTORS:
- Unprovoked PE at young age
- Family history of clots
- Recurrent miscarriages
- Known thrombophilia (history-level)

Q1. Prior clots?
A:
- “This is my second PE.”
- “Had DVT in the past.”

Q2. Family history?
A:
- “Mother and sister had clots.”

Q3. Pregnancy history (for women)?
A:
- “Multiple miscarriages.”

RED FLAGS:
- Recurrent episodes of unexplained SOB and chest pain
- Clot events at very young age

SECTION 11 — PE MIMICS & DIFFERENTIALS (SYMPTOM DISCRIMINATION)
===================================================================

MI (MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION):
- Chest pressure, radiates to arm/jaw
- Nausea, sweating
- Less pleuritic
- Often not positional

PNEUMONIA:
- Fever, productive cough
- Gradual onset
- Pleuritic pain but with infectious symptoms

PNEUMOTHORAX:
- Sudden unilateral pleuritic pain
- Decreased breath sounds on one side (exam-level)
- Often in tall slender or COPD

AORTIC DISSECTION:
- Tearing pain to back
- Neuro deficits
- Different BP in arms

PE CLUES VS MIMICS:
- Pleuritic pain + risk factors + tachycardia = PE higher concern
- Normal CXR with severe SOB and pleuritic pain → think PE

===================================================================
SECTION 12 — SYNCOPE AND SHOCK WITH PE
===================================================================

CLUES:
- Sudden collapse
- Very short of breath
- Tachycardia
- Hypotension
- Cold, clammy skin

Q1. Did you pass out?
A:
- “Yes, briefly lost consciousness.”

Q2. Before fainting?
A:
- “Felt very short of breath and chest tight.”

RED FLAGS:
- Syncope with SOB and chest pain
- Hypotension
- Low oxygen saturations

===================================================================
SECTION 13 — PE IN YOUNG “HEALTHY” PATIENTS
===================================================================

CLUES:
- OCP use / hormonal therapy
- Smoking
- Travel
- Recent minor surgery or injury
- Family history of clots

Q1. Medications?
A:
- “On birth control pills.”

Q2. Recent travel?
A:
- “Long car ride or flight.”

Q3. Symptoms?
A:
- “Sudden SOB and side chest pain.”

RED FLAGS:
- Sudden SOB in young patient on OCPs
- Pleuritic chest pain + tachycardia

===================================================================
SECTION 14 — PE AFTER COVID-19 OR SEVERE INFECTION
===================================================================

CLUES:
- Recent COVID or severe infection
- New/worsening SOB after initial improvement
- Chest pain
- Leg swelling

Q1. Recent illness?
A:
- “Had COVID a couple of weeks ago, was improving.”

Q2. Now?
A:
- “Breathing suddenly worse again.”

RED FLAGS:
- Worsening SOB after initial improvement from infection
- Chest pain + tachycardia in post-COVID phase

===================================================================
SECTION 15 — GLOBAL PE RED-FLAG SYMPTOM PATTERNS (CONSOLIDATED)
===================================================================
- Sudden onset shortness of breath, especially with:
  - Pleuritic chest pain
  - Tachycardia
  - Hypoxia
- Syncope or near-syncope with SOB or chest pain
- Unexplained tachycardia at rest + risk factors (surgery, travel, cancer, pregnancy, OCPs)
- Unilateral leg swelling or pain + new SOB
- Hemoptysis + pleuritic chest pain
- Shock signs (low BP, confusion, cold/clammy skin) with respiratory distress
- Persistent exertional SOB after prior PE (CTEPH suspicion)
- New severe SOB in cancer or postpartum patient
